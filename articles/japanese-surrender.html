The New York Times

Saturday, January 12, 1946

Japan Surrenders; Pacific War Ends

Allied Forces Complete Occupation of Tokyo

By William P. Collins, Foreign Affairs Correspondent

TOKYO — The Empire of Japan has formally surrendered to the United States and its Allies. After nearly five years of combat in the Pacific theater, Emperor Hirohito issued an imperial rescript yesterday ordering the unconditional cessation of hostilities.

The surrender was signed aboard the battleship Missouri, anchored in Tokyo Bay. President Cordell Hull announced the victory from the White House, calling it “a triumph of perseverance and the undying will of free nations to overcome tyranny.”

Emperor’s Broadcast Signals End

At 9:00 yesterday morning, Emperor Hirohito’s voice was transmitted by radio across the home islands. In measured tones, he instructed his people to “endure the unendurable” and submit to Allied surrender terms. This marked only the second time the Emperor’s voice had been broadcast publicly, the first being his 1945 call for resistance following Allied landings in northern Hokkaido.

The imperial broadcast was followed immediately by coordinated entry of American and Soviet troops into Tokyo. Columns moved from the west and south, securing government buildings, airfields, and communication centers. The occupation proceeded without incident. Japanese Imperial Army officers surrendered their swords in formal ceremonies throughout the city.

Streets Fall Silent

Tokyo’s streets, once filled with wartime activity, have fallen quiet. Shops are shuttered. Flags hang from windows, both Imperial and white. Residents have largely retreated indoors since the Emperor’s announcement.

There were no demonstrations or gunfire. Japanese citizens watched Allied troops with expressions of resignation and wariness, but also with notable civility. At one intersection, a middle-aged vendor selling rice balls offered one to an American lieutenant. The soldier accepted with a brief bow.

Near Yasukuni Shrine, elderly men and women lit incense for the war dead. Children peered from doorways as foreign soldiers passed. The city bears deep scars from years of bombing. Entire districts lie in ruins. Food remains scarce, and winter offers no relief.

Terms Include Joint Occupation

Details of the surrender agreement have not been fully disclosed. However, sources confirm Japan will undergo joint occupation under American and Soviet administration. Terms include demilitarization of armed forces, dissolution of the imperial government structure, and establishment of a democratic constitution.

A detachment of U.S. Marines now occupies the Diet building. Red Army units control military depots along the northern rail corridor. General George C. Marshall, arriving at Atsugi Airfield this morning, told correspondents: “The war in the Pacific is over. Let our conduct in peace be as resolute and humane as our conduct in battle.”

Allied commanders have issued orders emphasizing discipline and restraint among occupation forces.

Hull’s Cautious Strategy

The final campaign began with U.S.–Soviet landings in Hokkaido and Kyushu in mid-1945. Fierce Japanese resistance slowed the Allied advance. Coordinated pressure from both forces ultimately broke Japanese command.

White House sources say President Hull rejected several strategic options presented by the War Department, insisting that lasting peace could not be achieved through methods that would “disgrace the conscience of mankind.”

Hull appeared resolute during yesterday’s address. Administration insiders say he intends to remain in office through year’s end.

America Celebrates Victory

Across the United States, celebrations erupted overnight. Church bells rang from coast to coast. Crowds filled city streets. In New York’s Times Square, revelers waved flags and threw confetti. In San Francisco, veterans embraced family members.

“We can finally come home,” said Sergeant John Miller, stationed in the Pacific. “It’s been a long road, but we did it.”

The war claimed over 400,000 American lives. Senator Glen Taylor of Idaho observed: “There is peace, but it has come at great cost.”

Reconstruction Plans Ahead

President Hull stated that rebuilding Japan will be “long and arduous.” The United States will lead the occupation while cooperating with the Soviet Union and British Commonwealth. Initial reconstruction plans are expected at the upcoming San Francisco Conference.

The United Nations will convene an emergency session next week to address the transition to peace in Asia and Eastern Europe. The organization was formally chartered last October.

Though Emperor Hirohito remains in his palace, rumors suggest he will soon make a public appearance to affirm his commitment to peace. No formal abdication is expected.

War’s End Brings Uncertainty

The conflict began with Japan’s attack on Pearl Harbor in December 1941. Tokyo now awaits the outcome of the occupation and the work of reconstruction. The imperial era has ended, and Allied authorities are preparing to implement the terms of surrender. For the people of Japan and the world, the conclusion of hostilities marks the beginning of a difficult but necessary effort to restore stability and peace across the Pacific.
