From Moon to Mars: The Cooperative Renaissance in the United States, 1960–1980

University Lecture by Professor Naomi Velasquez, University of Wisconsin – Madison

Good afternoon, class.

Today we enter a truly remarkable phase in the history of the United States—one often called the Cooperative Renaissance. The years between 1960 and 1980 mark not only the maturation of the American socialist project but its transformation into a global beacon of cooperative democracy. Over these two decades, the U.S. consolidated its socialist institutions, responded to emerging geopolitical threats, pioneered major technological achievements, and further advanced civil and human rights.

We’ll break this period into five key themes:

  1. Political Leadership and Governance
  2. Global Decolonization and International Cooperation
  3. Technological and Space Achievements
  4. Social and Cultural Advances
  5. Cold War with TIIBASA and the Tensions of Progress

I. Political Leadership and Governance: From Hass to Hatfield

In 1960, the election of Socialist Eric Hass signaled a renewed commitment to democratic socialism after two decades of moderate Democratic leadership. Hass, alongside his running mate, Bayard Rustin, promised universal health care and deeper global solidarity, especially with newly decolonized nations. Rustin, a major civil rights figure and openly gay man, broke historic ground—though his sexuality received little attention at the time.

Within his first year, President Hass pushed through major health care reforms, expanding the Socialist Health Boards into a fully national, publicly managed medical system. He also launched the International Cooperative Exchange Program, offering technical aid to decolonized nations seeking alternatives to capitalist or centralized Soviet systems.

The vision was clear: build a world based on cooperation, worker empowerment, and peace—not domination or competition.

Hass's unexpected death in 1966 from a heart attack brought Rustin into the presidency, making him the first Black and first openly gay U.S. president. His leadership style was calm, moral, and principled, especially when he openly discussed his sexuality and long-term partner in a 1967 press conference—a moment that significantly advanced LGBTQ+ visibility in the United States.

Though he chose not to run in 1968, Rustin’s presidency left a deep legacy in health care, civil rights, and the arts.

That year, Democrat Richard Nixon was elected on a platform of "institutional integrity." Interestingly, Nixon operated as a moderate technocrat, committed to preserving the cooperative economy. The Republican Party, now a fringe group, was no longer politically relevant.

Nixon oversaw further consolidation of socialist reforms, introduced cooperative computing initiatives, and managed the emerging global crisis with a new capitalist alliance.

In 1977, Democrat Mark Hatfield assumed the presidency, marking a shift toward environmentalism. His Green Cooperative Initiative began transitioning the U.S. energy sector toward worker-owned renewable energy enterprises, a legacy that still shapes our energy grid today.

II. Global Decolonization and the Rise of Cooperative Internationalism

One of the most important shifts during this era occurred on the international stage. The U.S., in partnership with a reformed USSR, supported waves of decolonization throughout Africa and Asia. By 1965, with Portugal relinquishing Angola and Mozambique, colonialism in its traditional form had essentially ended.

The Cooperative Development Initiative, launched by Hass and expanded by Rustin, helped newly independent nations build cooperative infrastructure—schools, farms, and worker-run factories—without falling into either Western exploitation or Soviet-style state control.

This third path, rooted in democratic worker ownership, became a model for dozens of countries. It was especially influential in parts of West Africa, Southeast Asia, and the Caribbean, where American advisors helped establish locally governed cooperatives in agriculture, health care, and education.

Even within the USSR, decentralization continued. Baltic states and Kazakhstan voted for independence but remained part of a broader cooperative alliance. Western observers were stunned that the USSR could allow self-determination without violence, setting it apart from the authoritarianism increasingly defining capitalist rivals.

III. Space and Technology: Red-White Star and the Digital Leap

Perhaps no area better symbolizes this period than space.

The Red-White Star program, a joint U.S.-Soviet initiative, officially launched in the early 1960s. In 1963, Neil Armstrong and Yuri Gagarin became the first humans to set foot on the Moon—broadcast to over 500 million people. Together, they planted both nations' flags and declared a message of peace. Armstrong said: “We come not as conquerors, but as representatives of all humanity’s hopes.”

By 1976, as America celebrated July 4th, astronauts Andrea Hollis (U.S.) and Viktor Petrov (USSR) landed on Mars during Red-White Star VII, planting flags once again and delivering a powerful message of planetary unity.

Back on Earth, the launch of WORKNET in 1969 revolutionized how universities, cooperatives, and research centers communicated. Unlike the military ARPANET in capitalist countries, WORKNET was built on cooperative principles: open access, shared knowledge, and decentralized control.

By the early 1970s, the People’s Computing Initiative brought affordable personal computers to communities across the country. This helped democratize technology, improve cooperative management, and educate a new generation of tech-literate citizens.

When automation caused job losses in 1972, Nixon responded not by defending corporations—but by introducing the Technology Dividend Act, which guaranteed income support and retraining for displaced workers. This was the crux of America’s model: tech progress must benefit everyone, not just a privileged few.

IV. Civil Rights, Cultural Flourishing, and the 30-Hour Workweek

Domestically, this was a golden age of civil rights progress.

The National Health Guarantee Act of 1966 formally enshrined health care as a right. Later, the Supreme Court’s ruling in Martinez v. Cooperative Housing Authority guaranteed full cooperative rights for same-sex couples, expanding LGBTQ+ protections nationwide.

The National Cultural Cooperative Program, launched under Rustin, established artist cooperatives, writers' collectives, and cultural centers across the country. For the first time, artists and cultural workers had access to stable incomes and collective ownership—allowing creativity to flourish free from market pressures.

By 1974, America fully implemented the 30-Hour Cooperative Standard, following through on Eisenhower’s earlier labor reforms. This reduced the standard workweek while maintaining full wages, distributing the benefits of automation and productivity to all. It was more than a labor law—it was a philosophical statement: human life should be defined by purpose and community, not endless labor.

V. TIIBASA, the Free World Defense Pact, and Cold War Tensions

However, not all was peaceful.

Beginning in the mid-1960s, U.S. intelligence discovered a secret nuclear alliance of capitalist and authoritarian states—TIIBASA: Turkey, Iran, India, Brazil, Argentina, and South Africa. By 1967, India had tested its first nuclear weapon, with uranium and engineering help from other member nations.

By the early 1970s, this alliance emerged publicly as the “Free World Defense Pact,” claiming to defend “free market democracy” against so-called “socialist imperialism.” This bloc represented a direct ideological, economic, and military threat to the cooperative order.

The U.S. responded by modernizing its deterrent while still adhering to the Cooperative Defense Charter (1973), which banned unilateral military action without League of Nations approval.

To counter TIIBASA's propaganda, the U.S. passed the Refugee Protection and Integration Act, offering asylum and cooperative employment to dissidents fleeing repression—especially LGBTQ+ people, labor leaders, and journalists. This made the ideological stakes clear: America would fight not only with missiles and policies—but with moral credibility.

Conclusion: Toward a Networked Cooperative Future

As the 1970s came to a close, the United States stood at a crossroads.

The youth-led “True Socialism” movement began demanding an end to all market mechanisms, even within the cooperative system. They founded eco-communes and experimental collectives to test radical models of fully democratic economic life.

Meanwhile, everyday Americans were adjusting to a life of shorter workweeks, shared digital access, and space exploration. WORKNET had connected the nation, and personal computers had entered the home. America was no longer just a superpower—it was becoming a model of integrated, cooperative technological civilization.

By 1980, the United States had:

  • Achieved universal health care
  • Democratized computing and cultural production
  • Reached the Moon and Mars through peaceful cooperation
  • Established inclusive civil rights for LGBTQ+ citizens
  • Responded to authoritarian threats through multilateralism, not militarism

It was an era of hope, solidarity, and unprecedented transformation. And as we’ll see in our next unit, the 1980s and 1990s would bring even more radical change—with the rise of global cooperative networks, post-capitalist experimentation, and the birth of planetary democracy.
