The New York Times

Wednesday, October 18, 1927

Government Moves to Steady Trade; People’s Bank Extends Credit Amid Socialist Transition

Public Works Corps Expands, Socialist Reforms Prompt Caution in Foreign Markets

By a Staff Correspondent

WASHINGTON — Federal officials to-day confirmed that a marked slowdown in business has been observed in recent weeks, arising from the ongoing transition to public control of key industries under the Socialist government. Export industries, farm incomes, and factory production have all been affected by the market’s adjustment to the new political order. Despite these interruptions, government spokesmen are assuring the public that the situation remains under control and that measures are being taken to maintain economic stability.

The Department of Commerce reports reductions in factory output, particularly in export-dependent sectors, including metal works and agricultural processing. Rail shipments have slowed as new national schedules are applied. In several cities, merchants report a cautious tone in new orders. Uncertainties in European markets have exacerbated challenges faced by domestic producers. Farmers, for instance, are reporting sharp declines in the prices of wheat, cotton and corn, due in part to reduced international demand and heightened competition from state-supported agrarian economies abroad.

“The market is in transition,” said Secretary of Commerce Victor Berger. “These changes may take time to integrate. Our priority is to ensure stability during this adjustment period, both at home and in dealings with international trade.”

Foreign Pullback in Response to Socialist Policies

The decline in exports is also attributed to growing hesitancy among foreign buyers, in light of the Socialist administration’s nationalization of industries and increasingly protectionist economic policies. Many foreign investors have withdrawn from the U.S. in recent months, unsettled by the expansion of public control into sectors once dominated by private enterprise. The introduction of state-run industries, aggressive taxation of foreign-owned firms, and tighter import regulations have cast doubt on continued foreign participation.

“We have noticed a marked reduction in export orders, especially in steel and machinery,” remarked Henry T. Cullen, export manager in Detroit. “Investors are holding back, uncertain of what lies ahead.”

The People’s Bank Intervenes

Amid these developments, the newly established People’s Bank—replacing the former Federal Reserve as part of the broader Socialist reforms—has moved to stabilize the financial sector. In a statement late yesterday, the Bank pledged to continue meeting the needs of solvent institutions and supplying liquidity to markets affected by the slowdown.

“We are prepared to extend credit to banks and businesses in need,” said a senior official at the People’s Bank, speaking anonymously. “Although there is understandable caution, we are confident the financial system remains sound.”

Public Works Drive Accelerates

The National Labor Corps (NLC), central to the Socialist administration’s efforts to combat unemployment and provide direct public employment during this economic transition, has expanded significantly. Authorized earlier this year, the Corps now employs over 400,000 men in infrastructure works—from road and school construction to rail repairs.

“Our aim is to ensure movement and meaningful work for all citizens during this transition,” said Secretary of Labor Rose Schneiderman. “The state must provide for those affected by market disruptions.”

Economist Voices Mixed

While the government holds that the economic disruption is temporary, some economists express concerns over the reforms’ long-term implications. They warn that fundamental reorganizations, regulatory burdens, and the shift away from market–based practices might prolong instability.

“We are in uncharted territory,” Professor Josephine Barr of the University of Chicago noted. “The market faces not a normal recession, but a reordering of the entire economic system.”

Retail and Farm Sector Note Unease

Retailers in major cities report weakened interest in large purchases. Department stores say essentials remain in demand, but sales of furniture and luxury goods have dropped. Agricultural merchants report hesitant buyers amid price uncertainty. One Des Moines banker observed: “Farmers are renewing notes, but unease is growing regarding what lies ahead.”

Government Readies Further Action

Officials have said further measures may be adopted, including additional public works and financial assistance to stabilise key industries. The People’s Bank continues to affirm that credit remains available to sound institutions and that liquidity will be maintained. Though officials view the current situation as temporary, economic concerns remain widespread as the nation adjusts to its new economic orientation.

Trade and industrial output continue to realign under the Socialist reforms, and both private and public sectors await further developments.
