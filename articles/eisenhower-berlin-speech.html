June 6, 1955

Eisenhower Delivers "Peace and Justice" Speech in Berlin

President Highlights Reconstruction, Cooperation, and Civic Freedom

Berlin, June 1955 — The city rises from the rubble of a war that ended a decade ago, its streets a mixture of restored facades and the still-visible scars of bombed-out neighborhoods. Berliners move purposefully along avenues that have been widened and repaved, while cooperative councils oversee the rebuilding of public markets, schools, and housing. From the air, the city looks like a patchwork quilt of old and new, a testament to the immense effort of workers, unions, and local administrators over the past ten years.

Across Europe, the experiment in postwar reconstruction has taken many forms. Some nations have embraced fully cooperative economies, seeking to bind labor, management, and government into a single framework for social welfare and industrial productivity. Others have retained capitalist structures, yet have instituted strong safety nets to protect workers and families, demonstrating that the continent's future need not be dictated by ideology alone.

President Dwight D. Eisenhower's visit to Berlin has drawn international attention not only because of his presence but because the city itself embodies a larger question: how nations can rebuild, govern, and coexist peacefully after the devastation of global conflict. In an era when Europe still navigates the balance between cooperation and autonomy, Berlin is more than a city, it is a stage for an unfolding demonstration of political and economic experiment.

The United States, keen to demonstrate moral as well as practical leadership, has sent Eisenhower to affirm its commitment to labor dignity, equitable reconstruction, and peaceful collaboration. Crowds gathered in the reassembled Reichstag today included Berliners of all ages, visiting diplomats, and correspondents from around the world. The air was tense with anticipation, yet alive with cautious optimism.

As Mr. Eisenhower prepared to address the nation and the world, the central message of his forthcoming "Peace and Justice" speech was clear: peace is inseparable from justice, and social cooperation — whether pursued through socialism, mixed economies, or capitalist frameworks — offers the surest path to stability. Yet he was expected also to warn against authoritarian tendencies, reminding all that freedom, worker participation, and civic responsibility must remain at the heart of governance.

Eisenhower's "Peace and Justice" Speech

Reassembled Reichstag, Berlin – June 6, 1955

My friends:
I stand before you today in a city that knows something about getting knocked down—and getting back up again. Ten years ago, Berlin was rubble. Today, it's a city on the move. That didn't happen by accident. It happened because the people of Berlin rolled up their sleeves and got to work.

Now, I've been a soldier most of my life. I've seen what happens when nations try to settle their differences with guns and bombs. I've also seen what happens when people decide to build instead of destroy. Let me tell you: building is harder work, but the results last a whole lot longer.

We're here today because Germany is putting itself back together. Not because some general ordered it. Not because some dictator demanded it. Because the German people — in free elections — chose to reunite their country. That's how it should be done. That's the American way. That's the democratic way.

Now, I know some of our friends abroad still get jumpy when they hear words like "cooperation" and "socialism." Well, let me set the record straight: America isn't afraid of ideas. We're afraid of tyrants. There's a big difference between a government that serves its people and one that enslaves them.

I've worked with cooperative farmers in Kansas. I've seen union men build the greatest military machine in history. I've watched socialists and capitalists sit at the same table and hash out their differences like civilized human beings. What matters isn't the label on the system, it's whether that system respects the dignity of working people and gives them a voice in their own destiny.

The territories that France and Poland now govern? Their people voted. Fair and square. No armies marching in at midnight. No secret police knocking on doors. Just citizens making choices about their own future. That's what we fought for. That's what we'll keep fighting for.

But let me be clear about something else: we will not tolerate bullies. Any government — socialist, capitalist, or anything in between — that denies its people basic freedoms is asking for trouble. Any regime that puts the state above the citizen is walking down a dangerous road. We've seen where that leads, and we won't stand for it.

America believes in fair play. We believe a day's work deserves a day's pay. We believe workers ought to have a say in how their shops are run. We believe nations ought to settle their differences around a conference table, not a battlefield. And we believe that free people can solve any problem if they're willing to work together.

Berlin proves it. Look around you. This city was written off ten years ago. Today, it's a beacon of hope. Not because of any one system or ideology, but because its people refused to give up. They organized. They cooperated. They built something better from the ashes of something terrible.

That's the choice before us all: we can keep fighting the last war, or we can get busy winning the peace. We can nurse old wounds, or we can roll up our sleeves and fix what's broken.

I've made my choice. I choose the hard work of peace over the false promise of easy victories.

The German people have chosen unity through the ballot box. The European nations have chosen to rebuild together instead of going it alone. Now it's time for all of us to make the same choice: fair play at home, partnership abroad, and the patient work of making sure our children inherit something better than what we started with.

That's good common sense, my friends. And with our own determination, we're going to get the job done.

Thank you.
