The Washington Sentinel

October 14, 2013

“The Saavedra Incident”: Catastrophic Rocket Failure Unleashes Environmental Crisis
By Mariana Keller, Senior Environmental Correspondent

WASHINGTON — What was intended to be the final phase of a long-promised effort to rid the planet of high-level nuclear waste has ended in one of the most devastating ecological disasters in human history.

On Friday morning, just after 10:00 a.m. Eastern Time, a coordinated launch of 32 rockets carrying nearly four decades’ worth of stored radioactive and industrial waste ended in catastrophe. Eyewitnesses across Florida, Texas, and the Caribbean reported seeing bright flashes in the upper atmosphere, followed moments later by shockwaves and falling debris. Within hours, radioactive particulate was detected across a multi-thousand-mile radius.

The initial investigation by the Federal Disaster Office (FDO) and independent aerospace observers confirms that at least two of the rockets suffered critical failures just above 60,000 feet, triggering a cascading midair collision. The detonation and fragmentation of the launch vessels created an uncontrolled atmospheric dispersal of highly toxic materials.

According to preliminary models, the radioactive fallout has contaminated parts of the southeastern United States, northeastern Mexico, and stretches of the Gulf and Atlantic coasts, with wind patterns threatening broader distribution over the Caribbean basin. Emergency declarations have already been issued in Louisiana, Florida, Georgia, and Alabama, as well as in several Mexican states. Evacuations are underway in low-lying coastal communities where contamination levels have already exceeded safe thresholds.

A Privatized Catastrophe

The launch, part of the Global Remediation Initiative begun under the Lott Administration and restructured under President Edward Liu, was carried out by Novagen Aerospace, a private contractor selected in 2010 to handle the waste removal project. Under the terms of its contract—previously criticized for its lack of oversight—Novagen was allowed to consolidate multiple waste shipments and forgo federal inspection of launch vessels and payload security systems.

Critics had warned that the decision to jettison decades of accumulated nuclear and industrial byproduct in a “single-launch strategy” was reckless. The move was widely seen as a cost-saving measure championed by Liu-era deregulation and affirmed by his successor, President Jackson Saavedra.

While President Saavedra did not design the launch protocols, his administration signed off on the final phase of execution earlier this year, despite mounting concerns from environmental scientists and international observers.

“It was a ticking time bomb,” said Dr. Lourdes Hernandez, a nuclear physicist and former member of the Clean Future Commission. “We had argued for a gradual orbital disposal schedule with layered safety inspections. Instead, we got a firework display of poison in the sky.”

Economic and Ecological Impact

The consequences of what’s now being called “The Saavedra Incident” are staggering. Early assessments from the Department of Agriculture project long-term contamination of soil and water sources across multiple states. The fishing industry in the Gulf of Mexico and along the Atlantic Seaboard has been effectively shut down, with both Mexico and the Dominican Republic declaring economic states of emergency due to poisoned waters and the collapse of regional ecosystems.

A joint statement from the World Health Organization (WHO) and the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) called the incident “an unprecedented failure of international environmental responsibility.”

As of this writing, the White House has released a brief statement expressing “profound concern” over the accident and pledging full federal resources to mitigate the effects. However, bipartisan outrage is mounting in Congress, with multiple House members—including representatives from both parties—calling for a full investigation and the potential suspension of all privatized orbital waste operations.

Political Fallout Begins

President Saavedra, already under intense scrutiny for his administration’s response to what economists have begun calling the Great Recession, now faces mounting calls for impeachment from across the political spectrum. Representative Talia Moore (S–WA) introduced a resolution calling for an emergency congressional hearing, stating, “This disaster was not an act of nature. It was a failure of leadership.”

While the administration has promised transparency and cooperation with federal inquiries, critics argue it may be too late. In the words of Senator Marshall Greene (D–IL): “We trusted outside contractors with the future of our biosphere, and this administration gave them the keys.”

As mass displacement, economic shock, and international condemnation begin to mount, the full cost of the Saavedra Incident remains unknown. What is certain, however, is that the age of deregulated environmental outsourcing must come to an end.
